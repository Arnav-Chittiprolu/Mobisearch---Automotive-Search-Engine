An '''electromagnetic pump''' is a pump that moves [[liquid metal]], [[molten salt]], [[brine]], or other [[electrical resistivity and conductivity|electrically conductive]] liquid using [[electromagnetism]].

A [[magnetic field]] is set at right angles to the direction the liquid moves in, and a current is passed through it. This causes an electromagnetic force that moves the liquid.

Applications include pumping molten [[solder]] in many [[wave soldering]] machines, pumping liquid-metal coolant, and [[magnetohydrodynamic drive]].

==Working principle==
[[File:Electromagnetic ship.svg|thumb|right|400px|Schematic of an electromagnetic pump]]
A magnetic field ('''b<sub>rc</sub>''') always exists around the current ('''I''')-carrying conductor. When this current-carrying conductor is subjected to an external magnetic field ('''B<sub>ap</sub>'''), the conductor experiences a force perpendicular to the direction of '''I''' and '''B<sub>ap</sub>'''. This is because the magnetic field produced by the conductor and the applied magnetic field attempt to align with each other. A similar effect can be seen between two ordinary magnets.

This principle is used in an electromagnetic pump. The current is fed through a conducting liquid. Two permanent magnets are arranged to produce a magnetic field '''B<sub>ap</sub>''' as shown in the figure. The supplied current has a current density ('''J''') and the magnetic field associated with this current can be called "Reaction magnetic Field ('''b<sub>rc</sub>''')". The two magnetic fields '''B<sub>ap</sub>''' and '''b<sub>rc</sub>''' attempt to align with each other. This causes mechanical motion of the fluid.

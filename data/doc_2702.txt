[[Battery recycling]] of automotive batteries reduces the need for resources required for the manufacture of new batteries, diverts toxic lead from landfills, and prevents the risk of improper disposal. Once a leadâ€“acid battery ceases to hold a charge, it is deemed a used lead-acid battery (ULAB), which is classified as [[hazardous waste]] under the [[Basel Convention]]. The 12-volt car battery is the most recycled product in the world, according to the [[United States Environmental Protection Agency]].<ref>{{Cite web |last=US EPA |first=OLEM |date=2017-10-02 |title=National Overview: Facts and Figures on Materials, Wastes and Recycling |url=https://www.epa.gov/facts-and-figures-about-materials-waste-and-recycling/national-overview-facts-and-figures-materials |access-date=2025-11-25 |website=www.epa.gov |language=en}}</ref> In the U.S. alone, about 130 million auto batteries a year are replaced, and 99 percent of them are turned in for recycling.[https://batterycouncil.org/news/press-release/new-study-confirms-lead-batteries-maintain-remarkable-99-recycling-rate/] However, the recycling may be done incorrectly in unregulated environments. As part of [[global waste trade]], ULABs are shipped from industrialized countries to developing countries for disassembly and recuperation of the contents.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Fitzgibbon |first=Will |date=2025-11-25 |title=Auto Industry Was Warned For Years of Poisoning, Sickness From Car Batteries |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2025/11/25/world/africa/lead-battery-recycling-pollution-cars.html |work=The New York Times |language=en}}</ref> About 97 percent of the lead can be recovered. [[Pure Earth]] estimates that over 12 million people in [[Developing country|developing countries]] are affected by lead contamination from ULAB processing.<ref>{{cite web |title=Projects Reports |url=https://www.worstpolluted.org/projects_reports/display/65 |website=WorstPolluted.org |access-date=2016-02-18}}</ref>

ULAB recycling has been used as an example supporting the [[pollution haven hypothesis]]. In 2009, the U.S. [[United States Environmental Protection Agency|Environmental Protection Agency]] significantly tightened [[National Ambient Air Quality Standards]] for lead pollution,<ref name="Tanaka">{{cite journal |url=https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w29146/w29146.pdf |doi=10.1257/aeri.20210201 |title=North-South Displacement Effects of Environmental Regulation: The Case of Battery Recycling |date=2022 |last1=Tanaka |first1=Shinsuke |last2=Teshima |first2=Kensuke |last3=Verhoogen |first3=Eric |journal=American Economic Review: Insights |volume=4 |issue=3 |pages=271â€“288 }}</ref> which made domestic recycling more difficult and expensive, but did not prohibit companies from relocating the work to countries where environmental standards are low and enforcement is lax.
